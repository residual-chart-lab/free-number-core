# Free Numbers Note 01: Residuals Between Non-Aligned Compression Kernels

**Status:** research note / checkpoint  
**Version:** v0.1.2  
**Scope:** minimal quaternionic residual calculations, failed escape attempts, and the corrected observation-kernel no-go.

## Abstract

This note records a stable checkpoint in the investigation of *free numbers*.

The core result is not that free numbers are impossible. The result is more precise:

> A single observation or compression map cannot both judge local closure and witness residual persistence.

If a residual lies in the kernel of an observation map, that same map cannot detect it. However, the residual may still become visible under a second observation map that is **not aligned** with the first.

The current candidate location for a free-number residual is therefore not a value, but a mismatch between non-aligned compression kernels:

$$\Omega \in \ker m_n \quad\text{but}\quad I(\Omega)\notin \ker m_{n+1}.$$

Equivalently:

$$I(\ker m_n)\not\subset \ker m_{n+1}.$$

This is the present mathematical target.

---

## 1. Setup

Let

$$V=\mathrm{span}_{\mathbb R}\{i,j,k\}$$

be the imaginary quaternionic basis, with

$$i^2=j^2=k^2=-1,\qquad ij=k,\qquad ji=-k.$$

Let $B_n$ denote the real vector space spanned by length-$n$ boundary words

$$a_1|a_2|\cdots|a_n,\qquad a_r\in\{i,j,k\}.$$

We use reversed quaternionic compression maps

$$m_n:B_n\to\mathbb H,$$

defined by

$$m_n(a_1|a_2|\cdots|a_n)=a_n\cdots a_2a_1.$$

The reversal convention is not essential, but it fixes signs consistently.

---

## 2. The minimal two-letter calculation

Consider

$$B_2=\mathrm{span}_{\mathbb R}\{i|i,\ i|j,\ j|i,\ j|j\}.$$

Define

$$m_2(a|b)=ba.$$

For

$$x=\alpha(i|i)+\beta(i|j)+\gamma(j|i)+\delta(j|j),$$

we compute

$$m_2(x)=-(\alpha+\delta)+(\gamma-\beta)k.$$

Therefore

$$\ker m_2=\mathrm{span}_{\mathbb R}\{\, i|i-j|j,\ i|j+j|i \,\}.$$

The symmetric mixed term is killed:

$$i|j+j|i\in\ker m_2.$$

The antisymmetric mixed term survives. Define

$$\Delta_{\partial}=i|j-j|i.$$

Then

$$m_2(\Delta_{\partial})=m_2(i|j)-m_2(j|i)=ji-ij=-2k\neq0.$$

Thus:

$$\Delta_{\partial}\notin\ker m_2.$$

This is the minimal positive residual calculation.

The quaternionic anticommutativity kills the symmetric mixed term and preserves the antisymmetric mixed term.

---

## 3. The three-letter sign sector

For three-letter words, define

$$m_3(a|b|c)=cba.$$

On the distinct-axis sector generated by permutations of $i|j|k$, we get

$$m_3(e_{\pi(1)}|e_{\pi(2)}|e_{\pi(3)})=\mathrm{sgn}(\pi).$$

Thus the $S_3$-sign component survives canonically.

In this sector, the surviving component is

$$\Lambda^3 V.$$

This is a genuine quaternionic result: the all-distinct sector detects orientation.

---

## 4. Determinant / contraction decomposition

For pure imaginary vectors $a,b,c\in V$, one has the quaternionic product identity

$$uv=-u\cdot v+u\times v.$$

Using this, the reversed triple product decomposes as

$$cba=\det(a,b,c)+(c\cdot a)b-(c\cdot b)a-(a\cdot b)c.$$

So $m_3$ contains two visible sectors:

1. an orientation sector $\Lambda^3 V$,
2. a metric contraction sector.

For all-distinct orthonormal axes, the contraction terms vanish and only the determinant/sign part remains.

For repeated axes, the contraction sector becomes visible.

This decomposition is another positive calculation that remains independent of the later no-go.

---

## 5. The observation-kernel no-go

Let

$$\Phi:X\to Y$$

be an observation, compression, or collapse map.

If

$$\Omega\in\ker\Phi,$$

then $\Omega$ is invisible to $\Phi$.

That fact alone is only the definition of a kernel.

The actual no-go is stronger:

> The same observation map cannot serve both as the judge of local closure and as the witness of residual persistence.

Local closure is expressed by $\Phi$-agreement:

$$\Phi(x_1)=\Phi(x_2).$$

But residual persistence requires a distinction between $x_1$ and $x_2$ that is not visible to that same $\Phi$.

Therefore, one map cannot do both jobs.

> Judge and witness cannot be the same eye.

---

## 6. Non-aligned observations

A second observation map is not automatically invalid.

Let

$$\Phi:X\to Y,\qquad \Psi:X\to Z.$$

If

$$\Psi=F\circ\Phi,$$

then $\Psi$ factors through $\Phi$. In that case, anything killed by $\Phi$ is also invisible to $\Psi$.

This route fails.

However, if $\Psi$ is not aligned with $\Phi$, then $\Psi$ may detect what $\Phi$ kills.

A minimal toy example is

$$X=\mathbb R^2,$$

with

$$\Phi(x,y)=x,\qquad \Psi(x,y)=y.$$

For

$$\Omega=(0,1),$$

we have

$$\Omega\in\ker\Phi,$$

but

$$\Psi(\Omega)=1\neq0.$$

Thus:

> $\Omega$ may be invisible to one observation and visible to another.

The relevant condition is

$$\Omega\in\ker\Phi\quad\text{but}\quad\Omega\notin\ker\Psi.$$

---

## 7. Free-number interpretation

For free numbers, the second observation must not be chosen by hand.

The non-alignment must arise canonically from the quaternionic compression structure itself.

Thus the target is a sequence of compression maps

$$m_2,m_3,m_4,\ldots$$

together with insertion or extension maps

$$I:B_n\to B_{n+1}.$$

The central condition becomes

$$I(\ker m_n)\not\subset\ker m_{n+1}.$$

Equivalently, there may exist $\Omega\in\ker m_n$ such that

$$I(\Omega)\notin\ker m_{n+1}.$$

This means:

- $\Omega$ is invisible at level $n$,
- but its extension becomes visible at level $n+1$.

That is the current candidate location for a free-number residual.

> $\Omega$ lives in the mismatch between non-aligned compression kernels.

---

## 8. Earlier test: kernel mismatch under insertion

A key form of the test is

$$I(\ker m_2)\not\subset\ker m_3.$$

For example, take

$$\sigma=i|j+j|i\in\ker m_2.$$

An insertion map, such as

$$I_c(a|b)=a|c|b,$$

asks whether

$$I_c(\sigma)\in\ker m_3.$$

If there exists a canonical insertion $I_c$ such that

$$I_c(\sigma)\notin\ker m_3,$$

then the residual is not detected by $m_2$, but is detected by $m_3$.

This is not a single-value residual. It is a residual shadow produced by non-aligned compression levels.

## 9. Failed escape devices

The following attempts were tested and found insufficient as foundations.

### 9.1 External tag $\eta$

A hand-added tag preserves history, but only because it was externally attached.

Result: not canonical.

### 9.2 Infinitesimal extension $\varepsilon\mathbb H$

An $\varepsilon$-layer preserves extra information, but by enlarging the carrier.

Result: enlarged algebra, not intrinsic residual.

### 9.3 Rees variable $t$

A Rees filtration can distinguish degree drops, for example:

$$E_iE_jE_i=t^2E_j.$$

But $t^2$ records filtration degree, not full contraction history.

Result: useful marker, insufficient foundation.

### 9.4 Contraction trace

A contraction trace records which positions contracted.

But in longer words, contraction patterns are generally non-unique unless a rule is chosen or all pairings are carried.

Result: either non-canonical or Wick-like.

### 9.5 Reduction graph

A graph of contraction and reduction events preserves process information.

But if it merely records the original word and its reductions, it risks returning to $T(V)$ or free-magma data.

Result: useful logging, not yet intrinsic residual.

### 9.6 Free magma

Keeping

$$(ab)c\neq a(bc)$$

as bracketed words preserves process distinction.

But this distinction is free-magma distinction, not quaternion-specific by itself.

Result: process-preserving, not sufficient.

### 9.7 Decorated reduction graph

Decorating reduction trees with dot/cross events is stronger.

However, in the minimal tests, the decoration was determined by the bracket structure.

Result: stronger than bare magma, but not enough.

### 9.8 Dropping $\Phi$

If the observation map is removed, residuals no longer collapse.

But the measuring device is removed as well.

Result: persistence without witness.

### 9.9 Growth entropy

One may try to measure residual nontriviality by graph growth:

$$h(G)=\limsup_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\log N(n).$$

This is promising only if the graph-generation rule is canonical and does not presuppose $\Omega\neq0$.

If branching occurs because $\Omega\neq0$, and $\Omega\neq0$ is defined by branching, the definition is circular.

Result: promising as a secondary observable, not yet a foundation.

## 10. Corrected theorem

The stable theorem is:

> A residual cannot be both collapsed and detected by the same observation map.

More precisely:

> If $\Omega\in\ker\Phi$, then any observation factoring through $\Phi$ cannot detect $\Omega$.

However:

> A non-aligned observation $\Psi$ may detect $\Omega$ if $\Omega\notin\ker\Psi$.

Thus, the correct structure is not a single observation map, nor a hand-picked second map, but a canonical family of non-aligned compression maps.

---

## 11. Conceptual interpretation

This resembles a pre-observation structure.

A residual $\Omega$ is not an ordinary value.

It may disappear under one collapse map while casting a shadow under another.

This is analogous in form to incompatible observations:

- one observation can erase a distinction,
- another observation can reveal it,
- the underlying state is not exhausted by one collapse.

Thus free numbers should not be treated primarily as a value algebra.

A better current formulation is:

> Free numbers study residuals that live in the mismatch between non-aligned compression kernels.

Or shorter:

> $\Omega$ is not a value; it is a residual shadow between non-aligned observations.

---

## 12. Next mathematical target

The next task is to formalize a canonical family

$$m_2,m_3,m_4,\ldots$$

and insertion maps

$$I:B_n\to B_{n+1}$$

such that

$$I(\ker m_n)\not\subset\ker m_{n+1}.$$

If this holds canonically, then the free-number residual has a rigorous home.

If it fails, the theory must retreat again.

> One eye kills it. Another non-aligned eye may see its shadow.
